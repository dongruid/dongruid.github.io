About Me

I am a PhD candidate in Neural Computation and Machine Learning at Carnegie Mellon University, advised by Xaq Pitkow and Stefano Fusi (Columbia University). My research investigates the geometric properties of neural representations that enable intelligent agents to learn and generalize effectively.

My work is supported by the NSF AI Institute for Artificial and Natural Intelligence (ARNI), where I explore how principles from neuroscience and machine learning can enhance our understanding of both biological and artificial intelligence systems.

Research Interests

Representational Properties of Neural Codes

Investigating geometric properties of neural representations that facilitate generalization in natural and artificial intelligence. Intelligent agents create internal representations of the world to predict the future—I study what makes these representations effective for learning in novel situations.

Data Science Applied to Real-world Challenges

Applying statistical modeling and machine learning to extract insights from complex, high-dimensional data. Trained in computational neuroscience, I specialize in analyzing noisy but structured datasets. I am interested in using these skills to tackle real-world challenges in user analytics, marketing, and related domains.

Applications in Vision and Language

State-of-the-art vision and language models demonstrate remarkable capabilities across diverse real-world tasks. I explore how principles from neuroscience and machine learning can provide insights to better understand, enhance, and ensure the safety of these models.

Current Research Projects

Geometric Principles of Disentangled Neural Codes

Jan. 2023 – Present | NSF ARNI Institute

Developing theoretical and computational frameworks to understand how neural systems form disentangled representations. This work investigates the geometric structure that enables efficient learning and generalization across tasks.

Emergent Geometry in Visual Neural Representations

Jan. 2024 – Present | Collaborative project with Stanford University (Enigma Project)

Exploring how geometric structures emerge in neural representations of visual information across both biological and artificial systems. This collaborative work connects theoretical principles with empirical observations in vision models.
