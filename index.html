Dorian

PhD Researcher in Machine Learning & Intelligence

About Me

I am a PhD student at Carnegie Mellon University, where I investigate fundamental questions about intelligence from a representational perspective. My research focuses on sparsity encoding, Fisher information, and developing novel analytical frameworks for understanding how learning systems extract and process information.

My current work involves developing a marginal Fisher information approach to evaluate the information extraction capacity of learning processes, with applications to understanding disentanglement as an inductive bias and efficiency metrics in representation learning.

Research Interests

My research spans several interconnected areas at the intersection of information theory, machine learning, and cognitive science:

  • Information Theory & Learning: Fisher information, marginal Fisher information frameworks, information extraction through learning
  • Representation Learning: Sparsity encoding, disentanglement, inductive biases in neural representations
  • Mathematical Foundations: Differential geometry, manifold theory, dynamical systems analysis
  • Applied Mathematics: Dynamical Mode Decomposition, signal processing, analytical frameworks for complex systems

Recent Updates

January 2025: Developing comprehensive framework on "Principles of Intelligence from a Representational Perspective" with focus on disentanglement and information-theoretic foundations.

Fall 2024: Structured research paper on marginal Fisher information methodology, comparing oracle, dense-assumption, and L1-regularized decoding strategies.
